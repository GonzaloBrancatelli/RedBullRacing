Cars

RB1

Our maiden challenger was brought to life in 2005. With the distinctive dark blue paint scheme accompanied with the yellow nosecone and engine cover logo, which still features to this day, it was our first step into Formula One. The mighty Cosworth TJ2005 normally-aspirated 3.0 litre V10 engine powered the Team in its first season. With David Coulthard, Christian Klien and Vitantiono Liuzzi behind the wheel we finished seventh with 34 points.

RB2

Designing an F1 car takes around 18 months; designing an F1 team takes rather longer than that. The RB2 was the first car designed by Red Bull Racing but it took place during an era of change, with a big recruitment drive reshaping the technical department and the factory beginning to see investment. Raced by David Coulthard, Christian Klien and Robert Doornbos the RB2 put the first trophy in the cabinet when DC finished third in Monaco.

RB3

Adrian Newey was recruited by Red Bull in 2005, he arrived at the start of 2006 to take up the post of chief technical officer but his impact wasn’t really felt until the arrival of the RB3: our first Adrian Newey-designed car. While technology moves ever onwards, every subsequent Red Bull Racing car carries the DNA of the RB3 in various design details. Raced by David Coulthard and Mark Webber, the RB3 saw the Team move up to fifth position in the Constructors’ Championship, with a best result of third, scored by Mark at the Nürburgring. This car was a sign of progress: the RB3 wasn’t leaving anything on the table.

RB4

Superficially, the RB4 looked very different to its predecessor, although in terms of its underpinnings this was very much a continuity car. Developing and optimising many of the themes that first appeared on the RB3 while working hard to create greater reliability. The Team slipped back to seventh in the Constructors’ Championship. Mark Webber scored points in half the races in 2008 and, while David Coulthard only managed two finishes in the top eight, his third place in Canada was the high point of the season.

RB5

Every so often, F1 hits the reset button. It did so in 2009 with a reworked aerodynamic package designed to improve overtaking. 2009’s cars were built around a set of regulations developed by the Overtaking Working Group [OWG]. Adrian Newey, Rob Marshall and Team revelled in the scope of the project and produced a car that was a contender. The RB5 was piloted by Mark Webber and Sebastian Vettel. And it was Seb who gave the Team its first victory. The Team would go on to score five more victories, four more pole positions and six fastest laps during the season, moving up to second in the Constructors’ Championship.

RB6

Which is Red Bull Racing’s best car? The statistics would point to either the RB7, the car that took 18 out of 19 poles in 2011. Or the RB9, the car which took 13 wins from 19 races in 2013, including Sebastian Vettel’s record breaking nine-in-a-row. In the garage, however, there’s a certain fondness for the RB6. Driven by Seb and Mark Webber, it is the Team’s first championship-winning car, doing the double in 2010, winning nine races and taking 15 pole positions.

RB7

After a remarkable double-title triumph in 2010, the Team’s next car not only had to improve on the RB6 but also to successfully overcome a number of technical challenges and the arrival of a new tyre supplier. The result was one of the most dominant machines in Formula 1 history: the mighty RB7, a car that took 18 of 19 poles and won 12 races on the Team’s way to a second Drivers’ and Constructors’ Championship double.

RB8

While there weren’t a host of changes for 2012, the technical adjustments that did arrive were significant, both aesthetically and aerodynamically. Safety concerns over high chassis resulted in unsightly stepped noses, while relentless and increasingly complex exploitation of exhaust blowing was even more tightly controlled. Ingenuity is hard to repress, however, and the Team still found ways to exploit exhaust gases and uncovered key aero gains in other areas. And, after 20 rounds, seven wins and seven other podiums, the RB8 claimed a third successive Drivers’ and Constructors’ double for the Team.

RB9

Ahead of the revolution planned for 2014, Formula 1’s 2013 regulations were pretty stable. There were some significant changes, however, and the most important was entirely beyond the Team’s control. Tyre supplier Pirelli was tasked with delivering rubber to a different set of parameters for 2013 and the results were controversial to say the least. It resulted in a mid-season change and after a tricky start to the season the RB9 came alive. A record nine straight wins later and the Team were champions for the fourth time.

RB10

The 2014 season ushered in the biggest change to F1 cars seen in many decades. Chief among the alterations was a move to complex and costly 1.6-litre turbo hybrid power units and the arrival of the new engine signalled a huge shift in the balance of power on the grid. Hampered by reliability issues but more so by a lack of horsepower, the Team’s days of dominance were at an end. The RB10 took three wins and nine other podium finishes to secure second place in the Constructors’ Championship.

RB11

Following the seismic changes of 2014, the RB11 was born under relatively stable rules, with most of the alterations being carried out within the Sporting Regulations. There were subtle and impactful shifts, however, most notably regarding nose shape, which affected downstream aero, and a confirmation of changes to the rules regarding suspension systems. Once again hampered by a power deficit, the RB11 scored just three podiums as it took the Team to fourth place in the Constructors’ Championship.

RB12

Under a relatively stable rule set, there were few substantial visible differences between RB11 and RB12. But, while the 2016 car was an evolution of its predecessor there were some key changes, predicated by rule changes and by research. RB12 scored two wins, in Spain and Malaysia, 14 other podiums and one pole, at Monaco.

RB13

For the 2017, Formula 1 introduced a comprehensive set of rule changes designed to reduce lap time, improve racing and result in more aggressive, racier-looking cars. Aston Martin Red Bull Racing’s response to the new rules et was the RB13, which won three times – in Azerbaijan, Malaysia and Monaco – and scored 10 other podiums.

RB14

After the major changes of 2017, the following season was a period of relative calm, with the most significant changes coming through controversial additions and subtractions. The Team’s RB14 scored four wins, in China, Monaco, Austria and Mexico and nine other podiums, as well two pole positions, in Monaco and Mexico.

RB15

A crucial regulation change for 2019 specified a much simplified front and rear wing assemblies. The switch initially caused issues but once again rapid and relentless development made the RB15 a three-time race winner and double pole position winner by the end of the season.

RB16

Ahead of the major rule changes coming in 2021, the 2020 Technical Regulations were stable and most of the changes, such as access to an extra MGU-K and reductions in testing, being contained in the Sporting Regulations. As such the design of RB16 was all about refinement of lessons learned from RB15 and exploiting areas where gains were still on offer.

RB16B

2021 was supposed to be the dawn of a fresh start for Formula One, with a comprehensive redrawing of aerodynamic regulations set to usher in an entirely new generation of cars. Then Covid-19 arrived and plans changed rapidly. The new regs have been deferred for a year and 2020’s rules and homologated car designs are carried over for a second season. There are, however, a few tweaks and caveats. But what we have in 2021 is the RB16B and it’s ready to charge.

RB18

After a year’s delay due to COVID-19, the massive changes originally planned for 2021 make their debut in 2022. Tight financial regulations will constrain spending and revised sporting regulations will reshape race weekends. However, it's in the technical regulations that the biggest changes are occurring, as F1 ushers in a new generation of cars designed for closer racing and more action. Welcome to the RB18…
